Red Fort

The Red Fort, or Lal Qila, is one of India’s most celebrated landmarks and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Commissioned in 1638 by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan, the Red Fort served as the political and ceremonial heart of the Mughal Empire for nearly 200 years, until 1857. Its name comes from the massive red sandstone walls that encircle the sprawling complex, stretching over 2 kilometers.

The fort’s architecture is a splendid blend of Persian, Timurid, and Indian influences, reflecting the grandeur of the Mughal dynasty. Inside, visitors can explore grand halls like the Diwan-i-Aam (Hall of Public Audience) where the emperor met his subjects, and the Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience) famous for the legendary Peacock Throne. The intricate marble inlay, delicate floral motifs, and ornate jharokhas (balconies) reveal the exceptional craftsmanship of the period.

The Red Fort also housed beautiful gardens, luxurious palaces, and the royal baths. The Rang Mahal (Palace of Colours) was decorated with shimmering mirrors and vibrant paintings, serving as the queen’s private quarters. The fort’s layout follows the Islamic architectural style, with symmetry and balance playing a key role.

Beyond its historical splendor, the Red Fort holds immense national significance. Every year on India’s Independence Day, the Prime Minister hoists the national flag from the fort’s ramparts and delivers an address to the nation — a tradition that began in 1947.

Today, the Red Fort stands not just as a reminder of India’s royal past, but as a symbol of resilience and pride. Its walls have witnessed centuries of history, from imperial processions to the freedom struggle, and continue to inspire millions of visitors from around the globe.
