Ellora Caves

The Ellora Caves, located in Maharashtra, are one of the largest rock-cut monastery-temple complexes in the world and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Dating from 600 to 1000 CE, Ellora is unique for its coexistence of Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain monuments, symbolizing religious tolerance in ancient India.

There are a total of 34 caves, carved into the Charanandri Hills, including 12 Buddhist caves, 17 Hindu caves, and 5 Jain caves. Each group showcases distinctive architectural styles, sculptures, and symbolic carvings representing their respective faiths.

The most remarkable structure at Ellora is the Kailasa Temple (Cave 16), a massive monolithic rock-cut temple dedicated to Lord Shiva. It was carved from a single rock and is considered an unparalleled feat of ancient engineering and artistry.

The Buddhist caves feature prayer halls, monastic cells, and intricate carvings of the Buddha and Bodhisattvas, while the Jain caves are known for their detailed artwork and peaceful ambiance.

Today, Ellora remains a major tourist destination and a testament to India's diverse cultural and spiritual heritage.
