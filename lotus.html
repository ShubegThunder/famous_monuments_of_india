Lotus Temple

The Lotus Temple, located in New Delhi, is one of the most remarkable modern architectural landmarks of India. Completed in 1986, it serves as a Bahá'í House of Worship, welcoming people of all religions, faiths, and backgrounds to meditate, pray, or simply enjoy its peaceful atmosphere.

Designed by Iranian architect Fariborz Sahba, the temple is inspired by the lotus flower, a symbol of purity and peace in Indian culture. The structure is composed of 27 free-standing marble-clad "petals," arranged in clusters of three to form nine sides. The nine doors lead into a central hall that can hold up to 2,500 people.

True to Bahá'í principles, the Lotus Temple has no idols, altars, or rituals. Instead, it emphasizes unity, reflection, and harmony, allowing visitors to experience spirituality in their own way.

Surrounded by nine pools and lush gardens, the temple is especially stunning at sunset, when its white marble glows warmly against the sky.

Today, the Lotus Temple is one of the most visited buildings in the world, drawing millions each year for both its serene beauty and its message of universal brotherhood.
